The RevOps AI Brief: Issue #3

Hope you're having a great week!

This week is mostly builds: a Breeze deal-copilot that lives in Slack, a Claude + MCP flow that maps an org chart in minutes, and an access-management agent that handles the unglamorous side of RevOps. Before any of that, one honest question (borrowed from Aptitude 8) about whether your team is ready for these in the first place.

Let's get into it!

 

Foundations first: are you actually ready for any of this?

Aptitude 8 just put out a frank walkthrough of why AI rollouts stall, and the answer is rarely the model. Their CMO and team argue the prerequisites are boring on purpose: centralized data, written-down processes, and the right systems wired to support them.

The quote that stuck with me: "You have to have the data, the aggregated data, centralized data to be able to access it. The other big piece is the process. We invested a ton of time and money into our process about understanding how we actually get business done." (Aptitude 8, [1])

Tools: their own internal stack centralized on HubSpot, with open APIs and clean field definitions before any agent gets pointed at it.

Impact: they describe it less as a metric and more as portability. With foundations in place, swapping in newer AI tooling becomes a configuration change instead of a re-platform. (Aptitude 8's claim, [1])

Build it yourself (the diligence pass before you ship an agent):

  1. Pick one revenue process (lead routing, deal review, renewal triage). Write the current version down in plain English.
  2. List every system that touches it and whether you can pull/write data via API.
  3. Identify the top 3 fields the AI would need (and how often they're blank today).
  4. Decide who owns adoption and training, not just access.
  5. Only then choose the agent. Skipping steps 1 to 4 is how 80% of these projects quietly die.

Build 1: Breeze AI as a deal-room copilot inside Slack

HubSpot's April + May 2026 updates push Breeze AI into Slack so reps and managers can summarize deals, prep for meetings, and create CRM tasks without leaving the channel ([2], [3], [4]).

The RevOps angle: leadership conversations already happen in Slack. Putting deal context there (instead of asking everyone to bounce into HubSpot) closes the loop between "what's going on with Acme?" and the underlying record.

Tools: HubSpot (Sales/Service Hub with Breeze AI), Slack with the HubSpot app installed, deal-stage and meeting data already populated in the CRM.

Impact: HubSpot positions this as faster deal triage and fewer context switches for sellers and managers. Their own framing, not an independent study ([2], [4]).

Build it yourself:

  1. Install/upgrade the HubSpot app in Slack and enable Breeze AI in your portal.
  2. Pick 2 to 3 high-traffic channels (forecast call, exec-deals, customer-escalations) and give Breeze the right scope.
  3. Define a tight set of prompts your team will actually use: summarize this deal, prep me for this meeting, draft a follow-up task.
  4. In your weekly forecast cadence, replace one pull-the-report step with a Slack summary. Compare the two for two weeks.
  5. Document which prompts produced action vs. noise, and standardize the winners as a Slack workflow.

Sources: [2] [3] [4]

 

Build 2: Map any org chart with Claude + FullEnrich's MCP

Episode 12 of FullEnrich & Friends is a hands-on walkthrough of using Claude with FullEnrich's MCP server to find an ICP, map the buying committee inside a target account, score who to contact first, and draft outreach, all in one Claude session ([5]).

Nilan (FullEnrich's AI lead) showed it live on modal.com: Claude picked Decagon as a candidate ICP, returned a relevance-scored org chart, named the technical champion to lead with, and offered to enrich phones + emails on confirmation ([5]).

Tools: Claude paid account (Pro or higher), FullEnrich MCP server (custom connector URL), HubSpot or Salesforce on the other side for push, FullEnrich credits for enrichment.

Impact: Nilan claims roughly 3-minute MCP setup, real-time waterfall enrichment across ~20 sub-processors, and around 30% of data filtered out as unverified before credits get spent. Vendor numbers, not independent validation ([5]).

Build it yourself:

  1. In Claude, go to Settings > Connectors > Add custom connector and paste FullEnrich's MCP URL.
  2. Authorize once. Confirm Claude shows the connector as live.
  3. Try one prompt: paste your own product URL, ask for 3 candidate ICPs.
  4. Pick one, ask Claude to map the org and score who to contact first.
  5. Approve enrichment for only the top 3 to 4 contacts, then push to HubSpot/Salesforce from the same session. Review what Claude wrote into which fields before trusting it on volume.

Sources: [5]

 

Build 3: An access-management agent for your RevOps stack

Console pitches itself as "AI agents for RevOps" focused on the part of the job no one volunteers for: who has access to what across Salesforce, HubSpot, Outreach, Gong, and friends ([6]).

Reps request access in Slack, an agent checks policy, routes for approval, then provisions (and later deprovisions) just-in-time. It also surfaces anomalies and audit trails per tool ([6]).

Tools: Console, Slack, your GTM SaaS stack (Salesforce, HubSpot, Outreach, Gong, etc.), role + territory definitions.

Impact: Console's positioning is faster onboarding/offboarding for sellers and cleaner role-based permissions (so dashboards stop showing ghost reps and orphaned opps). Vendor framing, no independent benchmark yet ([6]).

Build it yourself:

  1. Make a one-page matrix of roles x tools x permission levels you actually want enforced.
  2. List the top 5 access requests RevOps/IT gets weekly. Those are your first automated policies.
  3. Pilot Console (or a similar agent) on one tool first (start with the riskiest, usually Salesforce or HubSpot).
  4. Mirror your existing approval chain in the agent before you change it. Trust grows when the new system reproduces the old one.
  5. Once stable, expand tool by tool, and route the freed-up RevOps hours into actual pipeline work.

Sources: [6]

 

Where this is heading: explainable signal routing

The pattern across this week's reading: AI is moving from score the lead to interpreting combinations of signals (pricing page visit + product usage spike + champion returning) and routing them with a human-readable reason attached ([7]).

That last part matters. A black-box router that hands a $500k account to the wrong rep destroys trust faster than a slow manual one. The vendors getting traction (Console, Breeze, the routing tools in Moxo's buyer's guide) all emphasize the why alongside the action ([6], [7]).

For RevOps, the job description quietly expands: less manual triage, more governance of the agents doing the triage. Defining what good routing looks like, what data the agent is allowed to read, and how exceptions get back to a human is becoming the work.
