The RevOps AI Brief: Issue #2

Hey, welcome to Issue #2 of The RevOps AI Brief!

This one came almost entirely from RevOps folks on YouTube this month (two podcasts, a keynote, and a couple of live build walkthroughs). One idea kept showing up from people who'd never met: your AI agents are only as good as the revenue data underneath them. The model isn't your bottleneck anymore. Your CRM is.

Below you'll find the grounding problem, three builds that get it right (including a peek inside Perplexity's own RevOps), and why this makes your job bigger, not smaller. Every build lists the tools, the impact, and how to build it yourself.

 

First, the problem: grounding, not hallucination

Connect your MCPs (HubSpot, Salesforce, Gong, Slack, Stripe), ask Claude a question, and you get a confident, polished answer. On the RevOpsAF podcast, Vasco's co-founder put it perfectly: it sounds like a business-school intern. Totally sure, often wrong. Everything looks on track in the weekly review, then you find you missed the number by 21%, all because a churn email never made it onto the deal.

His take: that's not the model hallucinating. It's a grounding problem. Claude reverse-engineers your data and does the most plausible thing on a CRM that wasn't built plausibly (think the "onboarding" pipeline still holding closed-won, or default source attribution you don't actually use).

Tools: your MCP stack (HubSpot, Salesforce, Gong, Slack, Stripe) + Claude, plus a grounding layer (a tool like Vasco, or your own data dictionary in BigQuery).

Impact: wiring in four things takes accuracy from an 11-30% coin-flip to roughly 98%. Skip them and you repeat the BI era, where Gartner pegged 70-80% of projects as failures.

Build it yourself: ground the data before you trust any agent.

  1. Identity resolution: the Gong caller, the Salesforce contact, and the Slack thread are the same person.
  2. Metric definitions: write down what counts as ARR, a source, a conversion.
  3. Plan wired in: 100K MRR is good or bad only against target.
  4. Shared memory: tag outcomes so you don't relearn every week.

Own this and RevOps becomes the one team that hands everyone trustworthy self-serve answers. Skip it and every rep spins up Claude and invents their own numbers.

Build 1: Inside Perplexity's own RevOps

Nathan leads GTM systems and ops at Perplexity (he was at Ramp before). He showed three "skills" he built in the last couple of months that run on their own every day or week, replacing work that used to eat hours.

The three skills:

  1. Voice-of-customer dashboard: two prompts over call transcripts plus Salesforce, auto-tagging discovery vs follow-up calls and surfacing weekly themes with what to do about them.
  2. Weekly deck-prep: every Thursday it pulls from Slack, pings reps to confirm in-month pipeline, and sets a cron job to fold their replies into the final deck.
  3. Nightly CRM-hygiene job: cleans account ownership, fixes Salesforce hierarchies, reconciles mismatched domains, and DMs him the errors with severity.

Tools: Perplexity (Computer/Max), Momentum (call transcripts), Salesforce, Slack, Snowflake (via Claude Code), Polytomic, Linear.

Impact: tasks that took teams hours every week now run themselves. Notice skill #3: even at Perplexity, the foundation is a data-cleanup agent. In his words, "if there's not good data foundations, the accuracy fails."

Build it yourself: start with skill #1.

  1. Point Claude at your call transcripts and CRM.
  2. Prompt one: tag each call as discovery or follow-up.
  3. Prompt two: summarize the week's themes and the recommended next actions for product and enablement.
  4. Once you trust it, add the nightly hygiene job.

Build 2: A self-refreshing ICP (Attention)

From Attention's CEO at SaaStr: your ICP goes stale fast, so build an agent that refreshes it for you.

Tools: your CRM (closed-won deals), call recordings, Clay or Deepliner (enrichment), Slack (the approval step).

Impact: you move from one broad ICP to specific, evidence-backed buyer profiles, with outbound that has to clear a 5% predicted reply rate before anyone hits send.

Build it yourself:

  1. Each quarter, pull recent closed-won deals and their call recordings.
  2. Have the agent extract firmographics, roles, and the real pain points buyers mentioned.
  3. Enrich those profiles with Clay.
  4. For each prospect, let it draft the email and predict the reply rate. If it's under 5%, it rewrites until it clears the bar, then drops it in Slack for you to approve.

Build 3: A CRM hygiene agent that earns trust

An indie builder (Maha) shared a clean reference architecture for the forecast-hygiene problem. The real deal truth lives in notes, emails, and Slack, not in your CRM fields.

Tools: CRM fields plus notes and tasks, email and Slack context, a multi-agent workflow (built with Codex), an approval UI.

Impact: managers stop reading every note before a forecast call. In the demo, a deal marked "commit" for a June 28 close got flagged high-risk: notes showed CFO approval missing and procurement needing 3-4 weeks, so the real date was July 15.

Build it yourself:

  1. Ingest deal context (notes, email, Slack) and map it to the right opportunity.
  2. Extract facts: next step, decision-maker, risk, timeline.
  3. Validate each fact against evidence, then score the deal's hygiene.
  4. Surface approval cards (field, current value, suggested value, the source quote). The human approves, edits, or rejects.

One rule worth stealing: AI recommends, humans approve, and every recommendation needs evidence. High-risk fields (forecast, stage, close date, amount) never auto-write.

Why your job gets bigger, not smaller

Jeff Ignacio used to think AI would shrink RevOps. After a year of building, he flipped. In his RevOps AF keynote he framed it with Jevons paradox: when steam engines got efficient, we burned more coal, not less. Make RevOps cheaper to run and the org just asks for more of it.

Five things that were impossible on human bandwidth alone are live now:

  1. Full pipeline coverage (every deal reviewed weekly, instead of only the loud 20%).
  2. Continuous win-loss (patterns in days, not a quarterly deck nobody opens).
  3. Scenario modeling (a coding agent running Monte Carlo on the quarter).
  4. Coaching the middle of the rep distribution, not just the stars and the strugglers.
  5. Continuous churn prediction.

Your role shifts with it: report-puller becomes signal-layer architect, rule-config admin becomes agent operator, generalist becomes evaluation specialist. Judgment is the moat. Ignacio's sharpest example: an MCP only pulls about 200 to 2,000 records out of the box, so you'd only catch a wrong analysis if you'd built that report by hand before.

Impact: Vasco's models put this at roughly 30% of top line ($1.2-3.4M for a $5-20M ARR company) when you move metrics like quota-to-OTE (4-5x up to 7-10x) and manager-to-IC ratio (1:5 to 1:10).

Sources: [5] [1]

 

Try this week

Two quick ones:

  1. Write your next agent as a job description, not a prompt. Pick a role (say "ICP Research Analyst"), give it a title, a cadence, the output you want in your inbox, and what the team stops doing. Point it at your cleanest data and run it.
  2. Got 15 minutes? Write down three metric definitions: what counts as ARR, a source, and a qualified conversion. It's the cheapest grounding you'll ever buy, and it's all that stands between you and 50 coworkers inventing 50 different numbers next week.
