The RevOps AI Brief: Issue #4

Hope you're having a great week!

This week's issue is heavy on real builds and real CROs. Anis Bennaceur (Attention) open-sourced his sales-brain stack, and a founder built a Monte Carlo pipeline forecast on top of it inside a day. Databricks' CRO walks through exactly how he uses his own AI tool (Genie) to run his daily RevOps cadence at $5B+ ARR. And there's a clean lesson tucked inside both: a lot of the "agent" hype isn't where the wins live yet.

Three builds you can copy, plus where this is heading.

 

Build 1: A Monte Carlo forecast on top of Attention's open-source sales brain

Last week Anis Bennaceur, CEO of Attention, open-sourced (his words) a dialed-down version of his sales-coaching stack [1]. Sam Nadler at Rise Labs spent a day building on top: he took Attention's per-call scores, ran 10,000 Monte Carlo trials across the open pipeline, and put the AI forecast head-to-head with what reps committed in the CRM. Result was a dashboard that not only forecasts revenue but also explains why a deal dropped (his demo flagged a fictional "Helios Northstar" as promising until a board-level vendor-risk gate was introduced two weeks ago).

This is the pattern: stop treating call summaries as something you read after the fact. Make them an input to forecasting.

Tools: Attention's open-source sales-coaching repo [1], a Monte Carlo simulation in Python, your CRM as the commit baseline.

Impact: In Sam's demo (his own fake data), AI forecast came in at $1.17M against a $1.44M rep commit, with deal-level explanations and a CRO-friendly "delta vs CRM" view [1]. He flagged it as a prototype, not production. Anis said on the show: "the whole Monte Carlo simulation, we've never done it, we've never even thought about it."

Build it yourself:

  1. Pull per-call scores (or sentiment + risk flags) for every open deal from Attention OSS, Gong, or your conversation-intel tool.
  2. For each deal, sample a close-probability distribution from those scores plus stage history.
  3. Run 10k Monte Carlo trials across the open pipeline, then compare the mean against the rep commit field in CRM.
  4. Surface deals where the AI distribution and CRM stage disagree most. Those are your forecast call agenda.

Build 2: A $5B CRO runs his daily RevOps cadence through one text-to-SQL agent

Ron Gabrisko (CRO at Databricks, $5.4B ARR, growing 65% YoY) sat down on Mostly Growth and walked through exactly how he uses Databricks' own Genie tool [2]. It's the cleanest "AI for RevOps" workflow I've seen described by a CRO on tape this year.

His daily ask of Genie, paraphrased: where is my pipeline weak, which customers might churn in this region, which programs would close the gap, and what's the predicted outcome if I run them. Genie writes the SQL, makes the predictions, and builds the charts off proprietary CRM data, then suggests programs and forecasts their results.

Tools: Databricks Genie on top of their CRM warehouse [2]. The same pattern works with Salesforce Einstein, HubSpot Breeze, or a custom text-to-SQL wrapper over your data warehouse (BCG flags this exact pattern as the move from "prediction" to "execution" in RevOps [4]).

Impact: Ron's own numbers (his claims, on his own podium): consumption revenue predictable to within 1-2%, every rep has a personalized AI interface that surfaces next-best customer + next-best product + recommended programs, and CFOs of their biggest banks now mandate Genie use top-down [2].

Build it yourself:

  1. Get your CRM, billing, and product-usage data into one warehouse (Snowflake / Databricks / BigQuery).
  2. Layer a text-to-SQL+narrative tool on top (Genie, Einstein Copilot, HubSpot Breeze, or a thin LangChain/Claude wrapper).
  3. Pre-bake three canned weekly questions for your forecast call: "where am I weak by segment", "who's likely to churn in the next 60 days", "what 2 programs would close my forecast gap".
  4. Push the answers back as CRM tasks so the recommendation actually gets executed, not just admired.

Sources: [2] [4]

 

Build 3: The boring agent that earns the right to do everything else (CRM autofill)

Easy to miss inside the Attention demo was the bigger lesson Anis kept hammering: "Coaching is a vitamin. People want to buy painkillers. And the biggest painkiller was filling the CRM" [1]. Every customer he talks to (he said he spoke with one of the fastest-growing AI-native scaleups yesterday) still leads with "can you fill our Salesforce?" Yes. BCG's June report makes the same point a different way: AI-powered sales assistants that automatically execute CRM updates are now the foundation that makes pipeline scoring, autonomous lead routing, and forecasting actually work [4]. Garbage in, garbage forecast.

Tools: Attention, Gong, or Clari for call/email capture, plus Salesforce/HubSpot as the destination [1][3].

Impact: Anis flagged that Attention captures every CRM field (not just summaries) including very specific ones (e.g. "date of the competitor's contract renewal") that an SDR will basically never log by hand [1]. Warmly's 2026 RevOps guide separately reports that Gong Forecast now tracks 300+ signals per deal pulled from calls, emails, and engagement [3]. None of that downstream signal exists if the fields are empty.

Build it yourself:

  1. List the 8-12 CRM fields your forecast model actually relies on (next steps, economic buyer, mutual close plan, competitor, blocker, etc).
  2. Wire an autofill agent (Attention, Gong, or your own LLM + Composio + Salesforce API) to write only those fields.
  3. Add a write-back log so reps can see exactly which fields the agent touched and why.
  4. Audit weekly: sample 20 calls, compare auto-filled values to ground truth, tune the prompts on the misses.

Sources: [1] [3] [4]

 

Where this is heading: in 2026, deterministic workflows are still the right default for RevOps

One bit from the Attention episode that surprised me, coming from an AI-native B2B founder: "In a lot of cases, deterministic workflows tend to work as well if not better than stochastic agents. So if you can actually automate a lot of your business through workflows rather than agents, even in 2026, then there's a lot of [cost] that you can save" [1]. The co-host agreed, said he had not found one RevOps use case where the agent SDK beat a plain workflow + LLM calls.

This matters because every vendor pitch this quarter (Hyperscayle's 12-month "RevOps AI transformation program", say, which sells exactly the autonomous-agent narrative [5]) wants you reaching for the agentic option first.

The RevOps move: map the workflow first (stages, branches, write destinations), then ask which steps actually need probabilistic judgment. Most of routing, scoring, hygiene, and reporting are deterministic with one or two LLM-graded steps. Save the agents for the genuinely branchy stuff (multi-threading outreach, deal-recovery playbooks, freeform research) where the path is not knowable in advance.
